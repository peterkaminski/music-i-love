# One More Kiss, Dear

From [[Vangelis]], *[[Blade Runner]] soundtrack* (composed 1982, officially released 1994). Vocal by [[Don Percival]]; lyrics by [[Peter Skellern]]; music by [[Vangelis]].

A deliberate anachronism inside a near-future film score. In *Blade Runner*, it plays diegetically — leaking from a speaker in a crowded neon-and-noodles street — so the song exists as an object *in* the world of 2019 L.A., not as scoring. That framing matters: the song is a ghost the city is still haunted by.

## Analysis

### The pastiche, and why it doesn't sound like pastiche

The track is doing a 1930s–40s torch-song impression: crooner vocal close to the mic, brushed-feel rhythm, sentimental lyric ("one more kiss, dear / one more sigh / only this, dear, it's goodbye"). That kind of exercise usually sounds like a costume. This one doesn't — and the reason is production, not songwriting.

What Vangelis does is layer the [[CS-80]] (the warm, breath-responsive polysynth that is the signature voice of the *Blade Runner* score) *underneath* the period arrangement. Not foregrounded — you don't hear "synth." You hear what sounds like a 78rpm record, except there's a slow harmonic glow in the pad that no 1940 studio could have captured. The period surface is real; the inside of the harmony is 1982.

### The vocal production

[[Don Percival]]'s vocal is the thing Pete flagged as spot-on, and it is. A few of the choices:

1. **Mic proximity.** Very close, almost confessional, with the proximity-effect bass bloom you get from a ribbon mic pulled in tight. That's a deliberate Rudy-Vallée / early-Crosby trick — the crooners were the first generation who sang *to* the microphone rather than projecting past it. The intimacy is period-correct.

2. **No vocal reverb of the modern kind.** Instead, a short plate-ish tail that reads as "room" rather than "effect." It sits the voice in a small space, like a hotel bar rather than a concert hall. Again — that's how 1940 vocals were tracked.

3. **Slight vibrato on sustained notes, placed late.** The vibrato doesn't start with the note; it blooms in after the syllable has sat straight for a beat. That's a torch-singer tell and Percival hits it.

4. **Diction.** Rounded vowels, slightly theatrical consonants — the "dear" at the end of "one more kiss, dear" lands with a soft final *r* that's more music-hall than pop. He's acting the period, not imitating a specific singer.

The net effect is that the vocal doesn't feel like a 1982 singer doing a bit. It feels like a recording that was found somewhere.

### Why it belongs in the film

A movie whose central question is what memory and love mean for beings with four-year lifespans opens space for a song whose entire lyric is *"one more kiss before we part forever."* It's not subtle, and it doesn't need to be. In a film full of replicants trying to hold onto time, a torch song about a final goodbye is the right background radiation.

The diegetic framing doubles the effect: this isn't Vangelis telling you how to feel. It's a song somebody in that city is playing. The emotional content is already out in the world.

## Notes

- The *Blade Runner* soundtrack had a famous release delay. The 1982 LP branded as the soundtrack was actually an orchestral re-recording by the [[New American Orchestra]] — not the film audio. Vangelis's actual score, including "One More Kiss, Dear," didn't get an official release until 1994. For about twelve years it was a bootleg-only love.
- [[Peter Skellern]] (British singer-songwriter, "You're a Lady") is the lyricist — which explains the slightly English-parlor sentimentality of the words. Not quite Tin Pan Alley, not quite music-hall; its own thing.
- [[Don Percival]] was not a film-music regular. He was a British singer Vangelis knew and brought in specifically because he could inhabit the period.
