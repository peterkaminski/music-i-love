# POP!

From [[Nayeon]], *IM NAYEON* (2022), JYP Entertainment. Her solo debut single, released as lead track from her first mini-album. Written by [[Melanie Fontana]], [[Michel "Lindgren" Schulz]], and [[Kenzie]]; produced by [[Lindgren]].

Nayeon is one of the three main vocalists of [[TWICE]] — same group as [[Dance The Night Away]]. POP! is her first outing as a solo artist, and the song is built around her signature bright, bubbly tone.

## Status

Pete's not sure he loves this one yet — on shuffle rotation, under evaluation. Logged here because it belongs in the family of songs he's tracked, and because it sits in a clear lineage.

## Analysis

### The architecture

Sugar-pop with a wink. Clean major-key track, bright synths, clap-and-kick pop pulse, and the titular hook — the literal sound-word "POP!" used as the rhythmic/vocal centerpiece. The song is playing with *bubblegum-as-text-and-subtext*: the candy metaphor is the lyric, the onomatopoeia is the hook, and the production reinforces both with fizzy synth blips and percussive mouth sounds.

The vocal sits in a higher, lighter register than most TWICE material. That's a deliberate solo-debut choice — Nayeon is showcasing the airy top of her range, the "cute" timbre rather than the group's mixed-register attack.

### Resolution points

1. **The "POP!" hook itself as resolution.** In most pop songs, the chorus lyric *describes* an emotion that the harmony resolves. Here the hook is a sound-effect — literally the sound of a bubble popping — and the song's structure is organized around delivering that sound as the payoff. Every pre-chorus build is a bubble inflating; every "POP!" is the bubble bursting. It's almost more sound-design than traditional pop writing, and it turns the whole song into a series of setup-and-snap gestures.

2. **The pre-chorus syllabic climb.** Before each "POP!", the vocal does a quick climb in short staccato syllables — almost cheerleader-chant territory. The climb creates just enough tension that the "POP!" feels like genuine release rather than just a gimmick repeating. The craft is in how short that climb is: stretch it too long and the song gets exhausting, cut it too short and the hook feels cheap. They found the right window.

3. **The bridge vocal feature.** After the second chorus, Nayeon gets a short moment of actual *singing* — legato lines in her middle range, stripped-back arrangement. This is the song showing you she can do more than the cute-voice hook; it's also a palate-cleanser so the final choruses don't feel like they've been going since bar one. Return to the chorus after the bridge feels earned rather than just scheduled.

4. **The outro — hook without vocal.** The song ends with the "POP!" figure continuing in the instrumental while Nayeon's vocal thins out. Like an echo of the joke continuing after the punchline. Consistent with the song's sound-design logic: the hook is a *thing*, not just a sung phrase, so it can exist without the voice.

### Why the Chewing Gum resemblance makes sense

Pete's instinct to hear [[Chewing Gum]] by [[Annie]] (Norwegian electropop, 2004, *Anniemal*, produced by [[Richard X]]) is right — they sit in the same lineage:

- **Candy-metaphor title** — both organized around a sticky-sweet object as central conceit
- **Onomatopoeic / sound-as-hook** — Chewing Gum has its smacking synth figure; POP! has its literal pop
- **Sugar-pop with a knowing smile** — neither is naïve bubblegum; both know they're bubblegum and play with it
- **Production density in short bursts** — twinkly, glittery, lots of small percussive elements crammed into tight spaces

The lineage: Annie (early 2000s Scandipop / electroclash bubblepop) → Robyn's "Call Your Girlfriend" era → K-pop bubblepop (Red Velvet's "Russian Roulette," Twice's "Heart Shaker," and solo outings like POP!). The DNA is *self-aware sweetness* — production that understands it's being cute and gets precise about it, rather than defaulting to earnest.

If POP! isn't landing for you yet, the suspicion is that the "POP!" hook may be *too* front-loaded — it's so sound-design-forward that it skips past a tune you can hum back. Chewing Gum has both the smack AND a sticky melodic phrase ("chewing gum, chewing gum, chewing gum…") that stays with you. POP! leans harder on the effect than on the melody, which is a legitimate choice but asks more of the listener to meet it.
