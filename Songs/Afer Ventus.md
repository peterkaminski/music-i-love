# Afer Ventus

From [[Enya]], *Shepherd Moons* (1991). Lyrics in Latin by [[Roma Ryan]]; music by Enya; production and vocal arrangement by [[Nicky Ryan]].

Title is Latin — *Afer Ventus*, the African wind. The scirocco. A south wind carrying dust and heat up over the Mediterranean. The lyric invokes named winds and directions — *Aeolus*, *meridies* — like a wind-rose chant.

## Analysis

### The architecture

Afer Ventus is built on the Enya trick of **stacked suspensions over a slowly walking bass** — voices piled a few deep, each one a major-second or fourth away from the next, so the pad is always quietly leaning on itself. The song lives between A-ish minor and its relative C-ish major, and it keeps flickering between them instead of committing.

### Resolution points — the close-your-eyes moments

A few places where the tension stored up in the suspensions is *spent* rather than just held:

1. **The first full "Afer Ventus!" call** after the murmured Latin-list opening. The opening has the voices sitting on a sustained ambiguous minor cluster — low, churchy, no downbeat telling you where "one" is. When the call lands, the bass *finally* steps and the suspended fourth above the vocal drops to a third. That drop is the first time the song lets you breathe. It's not a big triumphant resolution — it's more like someone exhaling in a chapel.

2. **The modulation up into the chorus proper.** The song has a habit of side-stepping — a chord you expect to cadence suddenly becomes the pivot into a neighbor key a whole step up. When the chorus lifts, it's this pivot-plus-vocal-doubling move. You feel the floor raise under you. That's one of the smile points — it's a gentle *lift*, not a modulation that announces itself.

3. **The long sustained high note toward the end of the vocal phrase**, where Enya holds it above the descending pad. The pad walks down through its suspensions — 4-3, 9-8, 7-6 — and every step down re-contextualizes the held note. The note doesn't move; the *meaning* of the note moves. By the time the pad lands, the held note has been a tension, a color-tone, and a consonance in sequence. That's the Enya signature — letting harmony do the moving while melody holds still.

4. **The return to the opening material at the end, but now "answered."** The song doesn't so much end as settle. The choral pad comes back in its original shape, but this time a supporting voice (one of the many stacked Enyas) enters underneath it on the *tonic* instead of the suspended fourth. The cluster you heard at the start as unresolved is now resting. Nothing else has changed — just that one voice is in a different place — and the whole piece feels at peace.

### Why it works emotionally

The whole song is about **wind and distance** — named winds, cardinal directions, invocations. Musically, it keeps you in motion: suspensions always pulling toward somewhere, key always about to pivot. The resolution points above are the rare moments the wind *sets you down.* That's why they make you smile and close your eyes: the piece has been carrying you, and briefly it lets you land.
