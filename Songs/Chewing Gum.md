# Chewing Gum

From [[Annie]] (Anne Lilia Berge Strand, Bergen, Norway), *Anniemal* (2004), 679 Recordings / V2. Written by [[Annie]], [[Richard X]], and [[Hannah Robinson]]; produced by [[Richard X]]. Second single from her debut album.

Annie is one of the great underappreciated pop-craft artists of the 2000s — a Norwegian indie-pop artist whose work sat at the exact intersection of Scandipop sugar, electroclash cool, and Richard X's early-UK-hyperpop production. She's not quite an indie figure, not quite a pop star; she's her own thing, and *Anniemal* is the document of that thing.

## Analysis

### The architecture

Electropop at its most precise. Bright synth bass walks underneath the verse, twinkly little high-register counter-melodies dart around the vocal, and the whole track pulses with a confident, un-rushed four-on-the-floor kick. Richard X's production fingerprint is all over it: Glossy but never sterile. Dense but never cluttered. Small percussive details — handclaps, shaker, a distinct smacking-synth figure — fill the middle without ever stealing focus from the vocal.

Annie's delivery is the crucial ingredient. She sings **slightly deadpan, slightly cool**, in a Norwegian-accented English that never tries to hide itself. That affect gives the candy production an adult wryness it wouldn't have with a more conventionally "pop" voice. The song is about being over it — and the vocal sounds over it.

### Resolution points

1. **"Chewing gum, chewing gum, chewing gum…" — the tag chant.** The chorus lands on its main phrase, and then the hook gets repeated three times as a falling tag. That repeated tag is the killer earworm — you can hum it without remembering any other part of the song. The resolution works because each repetition gets a slightly different timbral backing — a new synth layer enters, a percussion element drops out, so the same phrase keeps re-contextualizing itself. The melody stands still while the production walks around it, same technique Enya uses in *Afer Ventus* for much more serious purposes. Here it's a wink, but structurally identical.

2. **The smacking-synth "pop" figure.** There's a short synth effect that lands on certain beats — a kind of percussive click-pop meant to evoke bubble-gum smacking. Every time it appears, it punctuates the groove and resolves a micro-tension in the rhythm. This is the direct ancestor of what Nayeon's [[POP!]] leans on, except Annie deploys it as *accent* rather than centerpiece. The effect supports the song instead of being the song, and that restraint is why it still feels fresh two decades later.

3. **The pre-chorus spoken-ish break into the sung chorus.** The pre-chorus has Annie in a quieter, almost conversational register — lower energy, more direct address. Then the chorus opens up: fuller harmonies, more synths, the tag chant arriving. The gear-change from conversational to sung is the classic pop setup-and-release, but done with Annie's cool affect intact through both halves. She doesn't "turn on" for the chorus the way a big-voice pop singer would; she stays inside herself, and the production does the opening-up around her.

4. **The bassline as quiet engine.** Not a resolution point per se, but worth flagging — the synth bassline walks steadily under the whole song, and the way it lands on chord roots at the end of each four-bar unit is deeply satisfying in an unshowy way. You stop noticing it because it's doing its job so well. When it drops out briefly in a bridge moment, you feel the floor disappear; when it returns, the song feels anchored again. It's one of those parts that rewards headphone listening.

### Why it still works

Chewing Gum is 20+ years old and has aged exceptionally well. The reasons are craft-level:

- **The metaphor has bite.** "Boys are like chewing gum — they lose their flavor, but you can't get rid of them" is a complete thought, not just a cute title. The lyric *earns* the candy production.
- **The vocal affect is durable.** Earnest bubblegum dates fast. Deadpan bubblegum doesn't, because it's in on the joke and isn't trying to convince you.
- **The production was ahead of its time.** The sonic palette that became ubiquitous in late-2010s K-pop (Red Velvet, Twice solo work, Loona-adjacent) was already here in 2004 — glittery synths, smacking percussion effects, tight bright low-end, vocal layering as texture. Annie and Richard X were painting with the exact colors that would go mainstream a decade later.
- **Restraint.** The song never over-reaches. No bridge breakdown, no key change for the final chorus, no screaming ad-libs. It knows what it is and delivers exactly that, at full craft.

### The lineage

Chewing Gum sits near the head of a particular pop lineage:

- **Before it:** Kylie Minogue's *Fever* era (2001-02) — sleek electropop with knowing polish; Sugababes' "Freak Like Me" (Richard X's own, 2002) — electroclash sample-pop; Saint Etienne's 90s output — melancholy electro-pop with cool female vocals.
- **Contemporaries:** Robyn's *Robyn* (2005) — Swedish counterpart, same knowing-but-sweet DNA; Goldfrapp's *Supernature* era; early Lykke Li.
- **Descendants:** Robyn's *Body Talk* trilogy (2010) — the masterpiece of this lineage; Carly Rae Jepsen's *Emotion* (2015); K-pop's self-aware bubblepop wave (Red Velvet, Twice, Nayeon's [[POP!]]); Caroline Polachek solo work; a lot of hyperpop that doesn't know it's descended from Annie.

If you love Chewing Gum, you'd likely love — if you don't already — Annie's "Heartbeat" (same album, Röyksopp production, *very* different song but equally perfect), Robyn's "Call Your Girlfriend," and Jepsen's "Run Away With Me."
